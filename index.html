How does this grid bot work?

What it does

The grid bot places a series of limit orders across a defined price range, alternating between buy orders below the current BTC price and sell orders above it. When a buy fills, a sell is placed one level higher. When a sell fills, a buy is placed one level lower. The bot collects the spread between levels repeatedly as long as price oscillates within the range.

Prerequisites

  • Your Lighter private key must be saved in Settings before starting.
  • Your account must have enough available margin — the bot shows you exactly how much is needed before you can start.

Configuration

FieldWhat it means
Lower boundThe lowest price level. Buy orders are placed down to this price.
Upper boundThe highest price level. Sell orders are placed up to this price.
Grid levelsNumber of orders spread evenly between bounds. More levels = tighter spacing = smaller profit per fill, but more fills. Minimum 2.
Notional per levelUSD size of each order. $500 = each order controls $500 of BTC at that level's price.
LeverageMargin multiplier. Default 50×. Lower leverage requires more capital but is safer.

Capital requirement

Required margin is calculated as:

Required margin = (levels × notional) × IMR × 1.05

IMR = 1 ÷ leverage (minimum 2% at 50×). The 1.05 adds a 5% safety buffer. If you don't have enough, the bot shows the maximum notional per level you can use.

How orders are placed

When you click Start, the bot calculates evenly spaced price levels between your bounds. Every level gets a limit order — sells above current price, buys below. Levels too close to current price are skipped to avoid instant fills. Orders are sent one by one to Lighter with a 120ms gap between each, with a unique ID per level so they can be individually cancelled.

After all grid orders are placed, two bound stop-loss orders are added at the edges of the range (see Stop-Loss below).

How fills work

The bot listens to Lighter's WebSocket for your account's order updates. When a fill is detected, the filled level is marked and a counter-order is placed one level in the opposite direction — buy filled → sell placed one level above; sell filled → buy placed one level below. Fill count and estimated P&L update in the stats panel.

Stop-loss

Two independent stop-loss mechanisms run in parallel:

  • Exchange-side SLs: Two reduce-only stop-loss orders are placed directly on Lighter at lower −0.5% and upper +0.5% when the bot starts. These trigger even if your browser is closed.
  • Client-side SL: The bot also watches live price in the browser. If price crosses either SL level, it immediately cancels all grid orders, cancels both SL orders, and sends a market close order — then stops the bot with status "SL Triggered".

Controls

ButtonWhat it does
StartValidates inputs, checks capital, places all orders, starts fill detection.
PauseStops counter-orders from being placed on new fills. Existing orders on Lighter stay open.
ResumeRe-enables counter-order placement after a pause.
StopCancels all open grid orders and both SL orders on Lighter. Does not automatically close any open position.

Price ladder visualization

  • Green lines — open buy orders
  • Red lines — open sell orders
  • Amber dashed line — current BTC price (live)
  • Grey dashed lines — pending or filled levels
  • Red dotted lines — SL boundaries (±0.5% from bounds)

State persistence

The bot saves its full state to your browser's local storage. If you close and reopen the page while the bot was running, it restores automatically, reconnects both WebSockets, and resumes monitoring. Orders already placed on Lighter remain active.

How this strategy works
Entry: previous 4H candle closes below the 200 EMA, current candle is bullish (close > open) and closes back above the EMA — a reclaim from below. Entry price is the close of that candle.
Stop loss: set as a % below entry (adjustable below) — this distance equals 1R.
Take profit: tested independently at 1R through 10R, plus 20R, 50R, and 100R. Each TP level is a separate full simulation — trades aren't partially closed.
Cooldown: after a stop loss, no new entries for 20 candles. Only one trade open at a time. Trades still open at the end of the data are marked "Open" and excluded from win rate.
Bull Market Data: runs the strategy only inside the selected bull run window(s), skipping everything outside them. The 200 EMA stays continuous (computed on the full historical data) so it's accurate from day one of each window — only entries/exits are restricted to the chosen period(s).
Balance & P&L: set a starting balance and risk % per trade. Without compounding, each trade risks a fixed $ amount (risk % × starting balance) regardless of current balance. With compounding on, risk is recalculated as risk % of the balance at that point in time, so wins and losses scale with account growth.

Known vs. unknown — why this distinction matters
"Buy rumor, sell news" only makes sense as a label for events the market could actually price in beforehand — a scheduled FOMC date, an IPO listing, a halving block height. Events like China's 2021 mining ban, the Terra/LUNA collapse, or FTX's implosion were not on anyone's calendar — there was no rumor to buy, because nobody knew it was coming. Labeling those as "sell rumor, buy news" is a category error: what actually happened is a shock, followed by either a continuation or a recovery. The China ban specifically: BTC fell 30% from the prior close to the crash-day low, then bounced +41.5% to the next day's high — a fast, violent V-shape, nowhere close to the multi-month +120% rally to that cycle's later ATH that an earlier, looser version of this analysis had wrongly credited to this single event. A separate trap: the Fed's March 2020 emergency rate cut and QE isn't "building tension" — the Fed made a deliberate decision to step in and print money, a discretionary policy action. But it also isn't a standalone price shock either: BTC had already bottomed three days earlier and was recovering by the time the cut was announced, so that event is shown with no Pre/Post numbers rather than forcing a crash/bounce shape onto a move that belongs to a different date. The table below tags every event with an honest Known beforehand? column — Scheduled, Known date but outcome uncertain, Building tension (risk visible but timing unknown), Discretionary intervention (a decision-maker chose to act — a central bank, a military, a negotiating party), Surprise shock, or N/A for pure price levels — so the pattern label only ever claims "known bullish/bearish" when the event genuinely was foreseeable. For surprise shocks and interventions, Pre/Post are measured from the actual price extremes of the move (prior close → the event's real high/low, then that extreme → the next genuine reversal), not a fixed window — because a fast 1-day crash and bounce gets averaged into near-nothing by a 7-day window, which is exactly the mistake an earlier version of this table made.
Why the methodology differs by event type
For scheduled, building-tension, and outcome-uncertain events generally, "Move into" and "Move after" use a fixed 7-day close-to-close window — there's a genuine run-up to measure, and 7 days is long enough to capture real anticipation without bleeding into unrelated news weeks later. For surprise shocks and discretionary interventions, a fixed window is the wrong tool: a crash that bottoms in one day and bounces the next gets averaged into near-nothing by a 7-day window. So those events instead use the actual price extremes — prior close to the event's real high or low, then that extreme to the next genuine reversal point — exactly how a charting tool like TradingView would mark the move. The China mining ban is the clearest case this fixed: a 7-day window had shown it as -26% / +7%, when the real move was a same-day -30% crash followed by a +41.5% bounce the very next day. Multi-day shocks (Terra/LUNA, FTX) needed an extra check too — their labeled event date wasn't always the actual bottom, so the true low (sometimes 1-3 days later) is used instead — confirmed against real hourly data, not assumed from the daily candle's low/high alone. The BlackRock fake-ETF rumor caught a related bug: the original "fade" number had picked the day's low as the post-spike reference point, but hourly data showed that low actually happened before the spike, during normal pre-news trading — the real post-spike fade was a much smaller -4.8% over the following hour, not the -14% originally claimed. Every surprise/intervention event on this page has since been individually re-verified against real hourly candles to catch this same class of error. The three FOMC decisions (Mar 2022 hike, Sep 2024 cut, Dec 2024 hawkish pause) get their own hourly-precision treatment instead of the daily 7-day window: the Fed releases its statement at exactly 2pm ET, so daily candles blur the pre-announcement drift into the reaction. Pulled from real hourly data, "Move into" is the 24h close-to-close leading up to the announcement-hour open, and "Move after" is that open to the close 4 hours later, capturing the statement plus Powell's press conference. The Dec 2024 case is the clearest example of why this matters: the daily-window version of this table had shown a flat -0.9%/-0.8%, when the real hourly data shows price cratered from $104,805 to $100,000 within 4 hours of the statement — a sharp, fast drop the daily window had completely missed.
Is "buy rumor, sell news" actually true?
Mixing surprise shocks (Terra, FTX, SVB) in with scheduled events muddies the picture — those are crisis trades, not rumor/news trades. Isolating the 7 events that were known well in advance (Coinbase IPO, both Fed hikes/cuts, ETF approval, halving, Trump's 2025 inauguration): 4 of 7 (57%) show the strict pattern — price ran up into the date, then sold off after. The Coinbase IPO, the spot ETF approval, and the halving are the clean cases. Both Fed decisions (hourly-precision data) and the September 2024 cut specifically don't fit the pattern at all — price was essentially flat going in and modestly positive coming out, not a rumor-pump-then-dump shape. Biden's 2021 inauguration, measured on the same fixed window as the other non-Fed scheduled events, also doesn't fit the classic shape — price was already falling into and through the date, not running up and dumping. That's the value of forcing every event through a consistent rule per category: it catches cases that "felt" like sell-the-news in hindsight but weren't, once measured honestly.
What happens when expected news gets delayed?
Positioning happens on the expectation, not the event itself — so when a catalyst is delayed or falls through, the unwind hits exactly like a "sell the news" move, just triggered by absence instead of arrival. Live example: a US-Iran peace signing scheduled for Jun 19 2026 was priced in as a bullish tailwind; when it collapsed overnight after renewed airstrikes, $601M in crypto longs got liquidated in 24h — a 15:1 long-to-short ratio. Expectation alone is enough to build the leverage that later gets flushed.
Do market makers use these events to drive liquidity?
The evidence is mechanical, not purely about move size. Surprise shocks naturally produce the largest single moves (COVID's -48.6%, the Feb 2026 capitulation, China's ban at -30%) simply because shocks are sudden by definition — that's not a market-maker signature, it's just what a shock looks like. What scheduled events show instead is concentrated, predictable timing: everyone knows the date, so leverage builds toward it specifically, which is what makes the post-event reaction so often mechanical once the date arrives. It isn't "MMs reading headlines" — it's that leverage concentrates ahead of known dates as traders front-run the outcome, and once price breaks the consensus positioning, margin calls cascade. The June 2026 FOMC under new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh is a clean live case: BTC rallied +12% into the meeting on rate-cut hope stacked with Iran peace optimism — then the Fed came out hawkish, and $400M+ liquidated within 24h, nearly half of it inside the first 4 hours, with longs taking the overwhelming majority of the damage. Once that leverage is purged, price tends to react less to the next piece of bad news — there are simply fewer stops left to run.

The weekend-pump / Monday-Tuesday-dump thesis is not supported in this data — weekends are flat-to-slightly-positive on average and Monday is actually the strongest single day. The effect also flips direction year to year (Tuesday was the best day in 2023, one of the worst in 2024 and 2026), which is the signature of noise rather than a structural edge — with 7 days tested, ~1 "significant" result is expected by chance alone. Treat day-of-week as a minor tilt at most, not a standalone signal.
